# Mathematical Analysis & Table Guide: K18A2 Solver Symmetries and Orbit Reduction

This document provides a comprehensive mathematical breakdown of the concepts, tables, and results generated by the $K_{18}$ Perfect 1-Factorization (P1F) cyclic automorphism solver.

---

## 1. Vertices vs. Rows (Factors) Symmetries

A common source of confusion is the difference between the **18 vertices** of the graph $K_{18}$ and the **17 factors (rows)** of a Perfect 1-Factorization.

* **Vertex Action**: An automorphism $\alpha$ is a permutation of the **18 vertices** (an element of the symmetric group $S_{18}$). The cycle structures shown in the tables (like `(17)(1)` and `(16)(1)^2`) describe this vertex action.
* **Row Action**: Because $\alpha$ permutes the vertices, it also permutes the edges, which induces a permutation on the **17 rows** of the factorization.

### Examples

#### Case A: Cycle Length 17 (Order-17 Automorphism)
* **On the 18 Vertices**: $\alpha$ has structure **`(17)(1)^1`** (one 17-cycle and one fixed vertex, say vertex 0).
* **On the 17 Rows**: The orbits of the rows must have sizes dividing the prime order 17 (i.e., 1 or 17). 
  * A fixed row (orbit of size 1) is impossible because the single fixed vertex cannot match to itself (matching edges require 2 vertices).
  * Thus, all **17 rows form a single cyclic orbit of length 17**:
    $$R_1 \to R_2 \to R_3 \to \dots \to R_{17} \to R_1$$

#### Case B: Cycle Length 16 (Order-16 Automorphism)
* **On the 18 Vertices**: $\alpha$ has structure **`(16)(1)^2`** (one 16-cycle and two fixed vertices, say 0 and $f$).
* **On the 17 Rows**: The orbits of the rows must divide 16 (sizes $\{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$). 
  * To partition the 17 rows, the only possible orbit sum is $16 + 1 = 17$.
  * Thus, the rows partition into **one cycle of 16 rows** and **exactly one fixed row** ($F_f$):
    $$G[0] \to G[1] \to \dots \to G[15] \to G[0] \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha(F_f) = F_f$$
  * In the fixed row $F_f$, the two fixed vertices 0 and $f$ are matched to each other to preserve invariance.

## 2. Calculating the Orbit Representatives (The "Big Numbers")

The counts of **Orbit Representatives** (e.g., **2,027,025** for $L=17$ and $L=16$) represent the number of canonical permutations checked by the solver after applying stabilizer reduction.

### The Stabilizer Group $H_0$
Without loss of generality, we fix the first factor as the Lucas matching $R_1$. The stabilizer subgroup of $R_1$ that fixes vertex 0 is:
$$H_0 \cong S_2 \wr S_8 \quad (|H_0| = 2^8 \cdot 8! = 10,321,920)$$

### Calculations

#### Case $L = 17$ (Cycle structure: `(17)(1)`)
* Vertex 0 is fixed, and the remaining 17 vertices form a single 17-cycle.
* Number of 17-cycles on 17 elements is $(17-1)! = 16!$.
* Dividing by the stabilizer size $|H_0|$:
  $$\text{Representatives} = \frac{16!}{10,321,920} = \mathbf{2,027,025}$$

#### Case $L = 16$ (Cycle structure: `(16)(1)^2`)
* Vertex 0 is fixed. We choose which of the remaining 17 elements is the second fixed point (17 options).
* The remaining 16 elements form a 16-cycle ($(16-1)! = 15!$ options).
* Total permutations = $17 \times 15! = 16!$.
* Dividing by $|H_0|$:
  $$\text{Representatives} = \frac{16!}{10,321,920} = \mathbf{2,027,025}$$

> [!NOTE]
> For smaller $L$, the number of representatives is slightly higher than the simple division $\frac{16!}{(17-L)! \cdot |H_0|}$ because some cycles have non-trivial stabilizers in $H_0$ (meaning they form smaller orbits, increasing the number of orbit representatives).

## 3. Passing Permutations vs. Isomorphism Classes (Why they don't match)

A common question is: **Why does the number of passing permutations not sum up to the number of isomorphism classes?**

### The Answer: Isomorphic Redundancy
No permutations are filtered out during the solver run. The discrepancy exists because **multiple different permutations can generate isomorphic (or identical) Perfect 1-Factorizations**.

#### Example 1: The $L=17$ Case
1. The solver finds **17 passing permutations** of order 17.
2. For each passing permutation, we map it under 18 different vertex shifts to align the cycle union of its first two rows with the canonical starter Lucas matching. This yields:
   $$\text{Recorded Matrices} = 17 \times 18 = 306 \text{ matrices}$$
3. When these 306 matrices are run through the graph canonizer, they are grouped into isomorphic classes. They reduce to exactly **2 non-isomorphic cyclic P1F classes**:
   * **GK (Georges-Kotzig) Class**: Has an automorphism group of size 272.
   * **GB (Bryant-Maenhaut-Wanless) Class**: Has an automorphism group of size 17.

#### Example 2: The $L=16$ Case
1. The solver finds **80 passing permutations** of order 16.
2. When these permutations are normalized into matrices and canonized, they group together and reduce to exactly **3 unique isomorphism classes**:
   * **1 Class (GK Construction)**: Naturally contains a subgroup of order 16.
   * **2 New Classes**: Each has an automorphism group of size 16. The 80 permutations are redundant representations of these 3 underlying classes.

## 4. Identifying Class Types (GK and GB) via Automorphism Group Sizes

In Step 3 of the pipeline (`OutAutomorphismGroup=21`), the program computes the **full automorphism group size** for each unique canonical class. These sizes allow us to uniquely identify the mathematical families:

### A. The GK (Georges-Kotzig) Construction
* **Group Size**: **272**
* **Algebraic Structure**: $GA(1, 17) \cong \mathbb{Z}_{17} \rtimes \mathbb{Z}_{16}$ (the 1D affine group over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_{17}$).
* **Calculation**:
  $$|GA(1, 17)| = 17 \times 16 = \mathbf{272}$$
* **Occurrence**: Found in both the $L=17$ search and the $L=16$ search (as it contains subgroups of both order 17 and order 16).

### B. The GB (Bryant-Maenhaut-Wanless) Construction
* **Group Size**: **17**
* **Algebraic Structure**: $\mathbb{Z}_{17}$ (the cyclic shift group of order 17).
* **Occurrence**: Found in the $L=17$ search.

### C. The Order-16 Cyclic Families
* **Group Size**: **16**
* **Algebraic Structure**: Cyclic groups of order 16.
* **Occurrence**: Found in the $L=16$ search (yields exactly 2 new non-isomorphic classes).
